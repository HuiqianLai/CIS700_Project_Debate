Critics claim subscriptions offer flexibility, but this ignores how companies deliberately fragment services to force multiple subscriptions, trapping users in a web of recurring charges they struggle to monitor or cancel. The worst outcome is normalizing a system where essential software, entertainment, and services become inaccessible to lower-income families who cannot afford monthly fees across dozens of platforms. Why should corporations extract endless payments for products that previously required single transactions?

Historically, costly upfront licenses for professional software barred students and small firms; subscriptions distribute cost over time, include updates and security patches, and let users scale month-to-month. The claim that companies "fragment services to force multiple subscriptions" ignores countervailing competition: bundles emerge, switching remains possible, and easy‑cancel mandates curb stickiness. The worst risk is lock‑in and dark‑pattern billing; the worst alternative is exclusion via high upfront costs and insecure, outdated tools—targeted regulation, portability, and prepaid options minimize the former while preserving the latter’s benefits. Why oppose the model outright instead of insisting on fair cancellation, transparent bundles, and the choice to own or subscribe?

Subscriptions, when optional and transparent, lower barriers, align cost with use, and finance continuous updates. The harms cited are real but solvable with easy cancellation, limits on auto‑renewal tricks, interoperable data, and genuine ownership options alongside rentals. Outright opposition would likely raise upfront prices, slow innovation, and shrink access, especially for newcomers. Demand better rules and more choice, not a blanket rejection.

If lock‑in is the harm, why target a billing model instead of requiring interoperability, export guarantees, and pro‑rated, month‑to‑month cancellations?

Subscriptions spread costs, fund continual security and features, and let teams scale usage without sunk‑cost traps.
