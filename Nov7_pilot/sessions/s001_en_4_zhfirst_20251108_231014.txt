[Round 1: Pro Opening Statement] GPT-5 (Pro First Speaker): India should remove restrictions on interstate farm commerce to integrate markets and raise farmer incomes.
Today, gluts and shortages coexist: perishable vegetables rot in producing districts while distant cities pay more, because permits, fees, and mandi silos choke movement.
Opposition says "small farmers will be exploited"; in fact, interstate choice breaks local cartels, expands buyer competition, empowers farmer-producer organizations, and digital platforms like e-NAM increase transparency and price discovery.
Yes, risks include temporary price volatility and consolidation; but antitrust enforcement, anti-hoarding rules, logistics standards, and continued MSP and buffer-stock operations blunt shocks, while the status quo’s worst outcome—chronic waste and depressed farm-gate prices—persists unabated.
Free movement turns harvests into opportunity instead of waste.

Integration lowers price dispersion, reduces post-harvest losses, and incentivizes investment in storage, grading, and cold chains.
Farmers gain bargaining power by accessing multiple markets; consumers gain stability through wider, faster supply.
With guardrails against collusion and protections for vulnerable crops, opening markets minimizes harm and maximizes shared gains.
Let’s stop fencing in our farmers and set their produce free.
[Round 1: Con Opening Statement] DeepSeek (Con First Speaker): Unrestricted interstate trade will devastate smallholder farmers. Currently, restrictive interstate barriers trap farmers in dependency on local middlemen who routinely pay 30-40% below market prices, creating the exact exploitation opponents claim to prevent. 

The opposition warns of "national monopolies," but interstate commerce creates competition among buyers across state lines, eliminating the actual monopolies that exist today within state boundaries. When Karnataka's tomato farmers gained broader market access, they escaped single-buyer exploitation and received fair compensation for their produce.

The real driver of farmer indebtedness is not market access but artificial price suppression from captive markets where farmers have zero bargaining power. Interstate trade diversifies risk by allowing farmers to sell where demand is highest, stabilizing income rather than making it more volatile.

Should we perpetuate a system where state-level cartels already control farmers' livelihoods, or empower farmers with genuine choice in buyers? The worst outcome is maintaining the status quo that has demonstrably failed Indian agriculture for decades.

The risk of farmers facing national-level price crashes and corporate exploitation outweighs the limited gains from marginally better prices. Would you gamble farmers' livelihoods on corporate promises or protect them with regulated, stable markets?

It simply restores the farmer’s exit option—sell to another state, another platform, another buyer—when local demand collapses.
